  • Mechanisms to balance pay between male and female workers overall and individually (performance will be compared female to female, and male to male for purposes of pay, but the pool will be graded the same per rank per sex).
  • Billing system can track flows (gender, partner company, gig work), quick payout

  • Verifies employers and contractors live in the local area (or physically visit the market)

Loans are submitted into a queue and all banks are able to look at the loan and either request more information, offer to accept or pass - rather then the loan submitted to a specific bank to be directly rejected - this allows gig workers to request small amounts of money for various items without getting directly rejected by lenders (remove the personal interaction in the local lending process - but the local lender makes the choices via the system) Loan and grant performance history - were jobs taken for the skill after grants and loans, jobs at all? A local lender can participate to build up history with gig workers who may have a difficult time otherwise building up credit and reputation to receive a home loan. Local banks make loans to difficult cases when they know enough conditions, this is a place to help. A feature where an employer can flag a contractor to be notified if s/he requests a loan - which then could be backed (either anonymously or with attribution).

NEW FRESH TEAMS Promote “new” product development teams to originate with a quality “core base” (engine/code/product/app/branding/marketing). These teams should not be loaded down with out of date workers that have little incentive to reskill and weight down the salary pool. This is the core problem of large companies - the inability to “retire” out of date assets on emerging (competitve teams). PROCESS SCHEMEING core of process/technique is to remove “the requirement” of expert level talent at every point in the process. So any piece of the regular process (adding features - building, releaseing, branding, etc etc) - should be regular tasks and not require "talent" This is by no means to avoid acquiring talent - quite the opposite Emerging talent should get "just the skills they need" and work on features and competing in a market place, not figuring out what people did before them and how to slightly advance.... MVP TEAM vs PRODUCT LIFE CYCLE TEAM MVP teams need to feature talent focused on leading/bleeding edge technique and tools Product Life Cycle teams need to focus on features to compete in a specific market MVP teams require high experience - these require the top talent with experience of many years with an elite lead - to select and implement the best existing technology and identiy changes in technology offerings and upgrade regularly to stay at the very edge Product Life Cycle teams are best served with long term retained employees that gain high knowledge of the customers, market, product and new opportunities. MVP TEAM DESCRIPTION - WHY This is for investors to understand the differences in team makeup to understand the ongoing value in this product. TEAM REQUIREMENTS FOR MVP Resources will be used mostly on a temporary basis - so knowledge over loyalty at this point - if someone knows how to do something and will do it for a short term fixed price, do it. MVP TEAM VS PRODUCT TEAM Local and/or closely connected team members preferred. While rewards will be profit / production driven, the real motivation lies in the desire of the team to have a working/competitive product to sell to a market. The core qualification of these teams is a vested interest in acquiring and retaining knowledge on a specific industry and software package. Customer support isn’t just answering phones. WHY NOT ONE TEAM For the MVP team, value current skills over retention, in fact, combining the two might be too expensive to consider unless it’s a principal player. Don’t commit principal interest to these developers - they’re unlikely to stay anyways bleeding principal interest for a “fixed fee” service. One of the MVP developers “must” be a principal, or these doesn’t make sense. Also bricks and mortar during this phase makes very little sense, use whatever resources are available where ever they are available in the world to get things done quick/cheap/advanced - as needed. PRODUCT TEAM BRIEF Value retention here - over a longer haul the people wanting the system to work well for each other matters a lot more then how skilled they really are. The good people get bored and make lots of money, get them to help others do it. Bricks and mortar makes sense at this phase, the product needs to be generating revenue related to this, and a local focus. ATTRACT AND DEVELOP YOUNG PROFESSIONALS TO MVP TEAM We want to teach and collaborate on the newest and coolest techniques with the newest and coolist programmers. We can’t afford to pay people who don’t make the company money now and will likely leave before they do. So how do we work with this? --- on a per project basis While you’re young, learning and adjusting to ...hmmm.. Many other things. Lets set down some guidelines so we can both succeed on a regularly basis with an ability to end such relationship (If we’re only winning all the time - why would we??? … we’ll get to that). For one, we only finish projects. If a project is longer then a few months and you’re not personally vested, etc, then a certain phase or segment. The idea is here, we will bargain in training and payback with each segment. For example, there is an interface in angular 4, and the backend is firebase. You know firebase, but are a react guy. We’d budget in for you to learn angular, but at the same time we’d expect you to put in extra time to keep a professional schedule for us and a quality product (through and through - not just the css layer). So we’re committed to helping you acquire skills, but at the same time, we’re comitted to you using them for all of us to make money. So why the hell would you leave if we win all the time (and we do)? Well we pay what we can. There are companies that will pay much much more, especially once you have the indentifiable skills we procure around here. Guess what, if you’re going to make a lot of money somewhere else, or just some money here. Go make a lot, and god bless. Lets just stick to the previous, we’ll keep the committments short in time length (on the order of a few months), so you can wiggle out and go do what you want. This of course is focused on the MVP type of programmers that want to churn through the newest tools. If you want to learn only a few tools, and longer term commitments are more appetising - bricks and mortar type of commitments are probably superior - but also getting rarer and rarer as they pay a lot … for not much. So to summarize: Our goal is to partner with the “hungry” programmer who wants to consume the newest tools on a regular basis, get short turnover cycles, rewards for learning a new skill on the fly to mash with other existing skills. We’ll cut and size projects to fit skills and developers as possible - as long as they have potential. This is the focus of this company short term - which is to create MVP products. Longer term, team chemistry (if not make up - literally family oriented) will be key over aggressive and talented types. People wiling to commit a lot of hours and focus because the rewards will be split across a group of people favorable to them (employee owned). PRODUCT LIFECYCLE The MVP creation phase should run 3-6 months (with 4-6 weeks of actual billable work). The production and support phase should run 5-10 years (current platform functionality is stable).
